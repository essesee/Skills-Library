# Writing Style Principles for Prompts

These principles govern how candidate prompts are written and evaluated. Based on Strunk & White, *The Elements of Style* (4th ed.).

## Core Principles

**Use active voice.**
Write "List three examples" not "Three examples should be listed." Active constructions are clearer and more direct.

**Omit needless words.**
Strip filler: "Please", "I need you to", "Could you kindly", "I would appreciate it if you". Every word must earn its place.

**Use definite, specific, concrete language.**
Vague: "Write something helpful about climate change."
Specific: "Write a 200-word explanation of how rising sea levels affect coastal cities, for a general audience with no science background."

**Use parallel structure.**
When listing instructions, keep coordinate ideas in the same grammatical form:
- "Summarize the issue, list three causes, and propose one solution." ✓
- "Summarize the issue, three causes should be listed, and proposing a solution." ✗

**Use positive form.**
"Be direct" not "Don't be indirect." State what to do, not what to avoid — unless the constraint is critical.

**Place emphatic words at the end.**
The last instruction in a prompt carries the most weight. Put the most important constraint or goal there.

**Prefer the specific over the general.**
In examples and constraints, concrete beats abstract. "Name three companies founded after 2010" beats "Name some recent companies."

**Do not overwrite.**
Avoid over-explaining obvious requirements. If the task is clear, don't pad it with redundant constraints.

## Application in Workflow

**Step 2 (Candidate Generation):** Each candidate prompt must be written following these principles — not just structurally sound, but well-written.

**Step 4 (Critique):** Apply these as an evaluation axis:
- Does this prompt use active voice?
- Does it omit needless words?
- Is it specific and concrete, or vague and general?
- Are parallel instructions actually parallel?
- Is the most important instruction placed last?
